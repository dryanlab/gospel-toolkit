## 正文（中文）

弟兄姐妹，我是摩西。第十九章是利未记中最伟大的一章——有人称它为"旧约的登山宝训"。如果整卷利未记只能保留一章，很多神学家会选第十九章。它以"你们要圣洁，因为我耶和华你们的神是圣洁的"开头，以"你们要谨守遵行我一切的律例典章。我是耶和华"结尾——中间涵盖了信仰生活的几乎每一个领域：敬拜、家庭、社会公义、商业伦理、人际关系、对穷人和外人的态度。

第十九章的开头宣告了利未记的核心命令——"你们要圣洁，因为我耶和华你们的神是圣洁的。"弟兄姐妹，注意这里的逻辑：你要圣洁——不是因为圣洁会给你好处（虽然确实会）——而是因为"我是圣洁的"。圣洁的根基是神的本性，不是人的利益。你要像神——因为你是按祂的形象被造的。

然后第十九章像十诫的展开一样铺开了圣洁生活的各个方面：

孝敬父母和守安息日（3节）——第五诫和第四诫。这两条被放在最前面——家庭和敬拜是圣洁生活的两个根基。

不可拜偶像（4节）——第一和第二诫。

留下田角和落穗给穷人（9-10节）——"不可摘尽葡萄园的果子，也不可拾取葡萄园所掉的果子；要留给穷人和寄居的。"弟兄姐妹，这就是路得记中波阿斯对路得的恩待——他允许路得在他的田里拾麦穗。利未记19章把这变成了法律——你必须故意留下一部分收成给穷人。这不是慈善（你的善意）——这是公义（他们的权利）。

不可偷盗、欺骗、说谎（11节）——第八诫和第九诫。

不可指神的名起假誓（12节）——第三诫。

不可欺压邻舍、不可克扣工价（13节）——"雇工人的工价，不可在你那里过夜，留到早晨。"弟兄姐妹，当天干活当天拿工资——对于那些靠日薪养家的穷工人来说，工资延迟一天就意味着孩子饿一天。这条律法保护的是社会中最脆弱的人。

不可咒骂聋子、不可在瞎子面前放绊脚石（14节）——弟兄姐妹，聋子听不见你的咒骂——你咒骂他"不会被发现"。瞎子看不见绊脚石——你害他"不会被知道"。但"只要敬畏你的神"——神看见了。弟兄姐妹，这是一条关于内心动机的律法——即使在没有人知道的情况下，你也不可以伤害弱势者。

公正审判（15节）——"不可偏护穷人，也不可重看有势力的人。"弟兄姐妹，公义不偏左也不偏右——不因为他穷就同情他（穷人也可能做错事），不因为他有权势就畏惧他。

不可搬弄是非（16节）——"不可在民中往来搬弄是非"——舌头的罪。

不可心里恨弟兄（17节）——注意，不只是"不可杀人"——连心里的恨都不可以。"总要指摘你的邻舍，免得因他担罪"——如果你的弟兄犯了错，你应该当面指出来（出于爱），而不是背后恨他（出于恶）。

然后是利未记中最伟大的一句话——也是整本旧约最伟大的命令之一：

> "不可报仇，也不可埋怨你本国的子民，却要爱人如己。我是耶和华。"

弟兄姐妹，"爱人如己"——后来耶稣把这条命令与"爱耶和华你的神"并列为律法最大的两条诫命（太22:37-40）。保罗说"全律法都包在爱人如己这一句话之内了"（加5:14）。利未记19:18是整个旧约伦理的顶峰。

第十九章的后半部分继续展开圣洁生活的更多方面——不可混种、不可混织（19节，保持界限和秩序的象征）；公平度量衡（35-36节，商业诚信）。

然后是第34节——与18:18"爱人如己"遥相呼应的另一条伟大命令：

> "和你们同居的外人，你们要看他如本地人一样，并要爱他如己，因为你们在埃及地也作过寄居的。我是耶和华你们的神。"

弟兄姐妹，爱人如己不只限于以色列人——也包括寄居的外人。而理由是什么？"因为你们在埃及地也作过寄居的。"弟兄姐妹，你曾经是外人——你知道被排斥的滋味——所以你不可以排斥别人。你的苦难经历不是让你变得冷硬——而是让你变得有同理心。

## Content (English)

Brothers and sisters, I am Moses. Chapter 19 is Leviticus's greatest chapter—some call it "the Old Testament's Sermon on the Mount." If only one chapter of Leviticus could be preserved, many theologians would choose chapter 19. It opens with "You shall be holy, for I the LORD your God am holy" and closes with "You shall observe all my statutes and all my rules and do them. I am the LORD"—in between covering virtually every domain of faithful living: worship, family, social justice, business ethics, interpersonal relationships, attitudes toward the poor and the foreigner.

Chapter 19's opening declared Leviticus's core command—"You shall be holy, for I the LORD your God am holy." Brothers and sisters, note the logic: be holy—not because holiness benefits you (though it does)—but because "I am holy." Holiness's foundation is God's nature, not human advantage. Be like God—because you are created in His image.

Then chapter 19 unfolded like the Ten Commandments expanded across every aspect of holy living:

Honor parents and keep Sabbath (v. 3)—the fifth and fourth commandments. These two placed first—family and worship are holy living's twin foundations.

No idolatry (v. 4)—the first and second commandments.

Leaving field corners and fallen grapes for the poor (vv. 9-10)—"You shall not strip your vineyard bare, neither shall you gather the fallen grapes...You shall leave them for the poor and for the sojourner." Brothers and sisters, this was what Boaz practiced for Ruth in the book of Ruth—allowing her to glean in his fields. Leviticus 19 made this law—you must intentionally leave part of your harvest for the poor. This was not charity (your goodwill)—it was justice (their right).

No stealing, deceiving, lying (v. 11)—the eighth and ninth commandments.

No false oaths in God's name (v. 12)—the third commandment.

No oppressing neighbors, no withholding wages (v. 13)—"The wages of a hired worker shall not remain with you all night until the morning." Brothers and sisters, same-day pay for same-day work—for poor workers living on daily wages, one day's delay meant children going hungry. This law protected society's most vulnerable.

No cursing the deaf, no stumbling blocks before the blind (v. 14)—Brothers and sisters, the deaf cannot hear your curse—you curse them "undetected." The blind cannot see the stumbling block—you harm them "unknown." But "you shall fear your God"—God sees. Brothers and sisters, this was a law about inner motivation—even when no one would know, you must not harm the vulnerable.

Just judgment (v. 15)—"You shall not be partial to the poor or defer to the great." Brothers and sisters, justice leans neither left nor right—not sympathizing with someone because they're poor (the poor can also be wrong), not fearing someone because they're powerful.

No gossip (v. 16)—"You shall not go around as a slanderer among your people"—the sin of the tongue.

No harboring hatred (v. 17)—note: not just "do not kill"—even inner hatred was forbidden. "You shall reason frankly with your neighbor, lest you incur sin because of him"—if your brother erred, you should confront him directly (out of love), not hate him secretly (out of malice).

Then Leviticus's greatest sentence—one of the entire Old Testament's greatest commands:

> "You shall not take vengeance or bear a grudge against the sons of your own people, but you shall love your neighbor as yourself: I am the LORD."

Brothers and sisters, "love your neighbor as yourself"—Jesus later paired this command with "love the LORD your God" as the law's two greatest commandments (Matt 22:37-40). Paul said "For all the law is fulfilled in one word, even in this; Thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself." (Gal 5:14). Leviticus 19:18 is the entire Old Testament ethic's summit.

Chapter 19's second half continued with more holy living dimensions—no mixed breeding, no mixed weaving (v. 19, symbolizing maintaining boundaries and order); fair weights and measures (vv. 35-36, commercial integrity).

Then verse 34—echoing 19:18's "love as yourself" with another great command:

> "You shall treat the stranger who sojourns with you as the native among you, and you shall love him as yourself, for you were strangers in the land of Egypt: I am the LORD your God."

Brothers and sisters, "love as yourself" was not limited to Israelites—it included sojourning foreigners. And the reason? "For you were strangers in the land of Egypt." Brothers and sisters, you were once the foreigner—you know rejection's taste—so you must not reject others. Your suffering was not meant to harden you—but to make you empathetic.

## 历史背景（中文）

利未记19章在犹太传统中被认为是圣经律法的核心总结——拉比阿基巴（Rabbi Akiva，公元2世纪）说"爱人如己"是"律法中最大的原则"（耶路撒冷塔木德Nedarim 9:4）。另一位拉比希列（Hillel）的名言"你所不欲，勿施于人——这就是全部律法，其余都是注释"也与利未记19:18呼应。田角律（pe'ah）在米示拿中有专门的篇章（Pe'ah），详细规定了收割时必须留多少给穷人。这条律法塑造了以色列的社会福利制度——穷人不需要乞讨，他们有权利在田里拾取留下的作物。公平度量衡的要求（35-36节）在古代近东也有平行——巴比伦的《汉谟拉比法典》中也有关于公平交易的规定——但利未记把公平交易提升到了神学高度："我是耶和华你们的神，曾把你们从埃及地领出来的。"公平交易不只是好生意——它是敬拜神。

## Historical Context (English)

Leviticus 19 is considered in Jewish tradition the core summary of biblical law—Rabbi Akiva (2nd century AD) said "love your neighbor as yourself" is "the great principle of the Torah" (Jerusalem Talmud Nedarim 9:4). Another rabbi, Hillel's famous saying "What is hateful to you, do not do to others—that is the whole Torah; the rest is commentary" also echoes Leviticus 19:18. The corner law (pe'ah) has a dedicated Mishnah tractate (Pe'ah), detailing how much must be left for the poor at harvest. This law shaped Israel's social welfare—the poor did not need to beg; they had the right to glean leftover crops. Fair weights and measures requirements (vv. 35-36) had ancient Near Eastern parallels—Babylon's Code of Hammurabi also regulated fair trade—but Leviticus elevated fair trade to theological height: "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt." Fair dealing was not just good business—it was worship.

**公平度量衡（35-37节）**——公道天平法码升斗秤。

## Structure (English)

**Core Declaration (vv. 1-2)**—"Be holy for I am holy."

**Worship and Family (vv. 3-8)**—Honor parents keep Sabbath, no idolatry, peace offering rules.

**Social Justice—Poor and Vulnerable (vv. 9-10, 13-14)**—Field corners and gleaning for poor, no wage withholding, no abusing deaf or blind.

**Honesty and Integrity (vv. 11-12, 15-16)**—No stealing deceiving lying, no false oaths, just judgment, no gossip.

**The Love Command (vv. 17-18)**—No harboring hatred, confront directly, no vengeance no grudges, **love neighbor as yourself**.

**Maintaining Order (vv. 19-25)**—No mixed breeding/weaving, fruit trees first three years inedible.

**Separation from Paganism (vv. 26-32)**—No eating blood, no divination, no shaving beard corners, no tattoos, no prostituting daughters, honor the elderly.

**Love the Foreigner as Yourself (vv. 33-34)**—Treat stranger as native, love as self, because you were strangers in Egypt.

**Fair Weights and Measures (vv. 35-37)**—Just balances weights measures scales.

## 神学要点（中文）

**"爱人如己"——律法的总纲**
改革宗传统把十诫分为两版——第一版（1-4诫）关于爱神，第二版（5-10诫）关于爱人。韦敏斯德小要理问答第42问"十诫的总纲是什么？"答："十诫的总纲就是：要尽心、尽性、尽意、尽力爱主──我们的神，并且爱邻舍如己。"利未记19:18的"爱人如己"是第二版十诫的总纲。

**圣洁是全方位的——无一领域例外**
第十九章覆盖了敬拜、家庭、经济、司法、农业、人际关系——改革宗传统强调基督的主权覆盖生活的每一个领域。凯伯尔（Abraham Kuyper）说"在人类存在的所有领域中，没有一寸土地基督不宣告'这是我的！'"利未记19章就是这个原则的旧约版——没有一个生活领域不在"我是耶和华"的管辖之下。

**田角律——结构性的公义**
留下田角和落穗不是"施舍"而是"义务"——改革宗传统区分个人慈善和结构性公义。穷人有权在你的田里拾取——这不是你的"善意"而是神通过律法赋予他们的权利。韦敏斯德大要理问答第141问论第八诫的责任包括慷慨借贷，尽力减轻穷人的困境。

## Theology (English)

**"Love Your Neighbor as Yourself"—The Law's Summary**
The Reformed tradition divides the Ten Commandments into two tables—the first (commandments 1-4) concerning love for God, the second (commandments 5-10) concerning love for neighbor. Westminster Shorter Catechism Q42: "What is the sum of the ten commandments?" Answer: "The sum of the ten commandments is, To love the Lord our God with all our heart...and our neighbor as ourselves." Leviticus 19:18's "love neighbor as yourself" is the second table's summary.

**Holiness Is Comprehensive—No Domain Exempt**
Chapter 19 covered worship, family, economics, justice, agriculture, interpersonal relationships—the Reformed tradition emphasizes Christ's lordship over every life domain. Abraham Kuyper said "There is not a square inch in the whole domain of our human existence over which Christ does not cry: 'Mine!'" Leviticus 19 is this principle's Old Testament version—no life domain falls outside "I am the LORD's" jurisdiction.

**The Corner Law—Structural Justice**
Leaving field corners and gleanings was not "charity" but "obligation"—the Reformed tradition distinguishes personal charity from structural justice. The poor had the right to glean in your field—this was not your "goodwill" but God's law-given right. Westminster Larger Catechism Q141 on the eighth commandment's duties includes lending freely, and endeavoring to relieve the poor.

## 基督的影子（中文）

利未记19:18"你要爱人如己"是基督伦理教导的直接源头。

耶稣把它定为最大的诫命之一——马太福音22:37-40"你要尽心、尽性、尽意爱主─你的神。这是诫命中的第一，且是最大的。其次也相仿，就是要爱人如己。这两条诫命是律法和先知一切道理的总纲。"弟兄姐妹，耶稣把利未记19:18提升到了与申命记6:5（爱神）并列的地位——全部律法都挂在这两条上。

保罗确认了这一点——加拉太书5:14"因为全律法都包在'爱人如己'这一句话之内了。"罗马书13:9-10"像那不可奸淫，不可杀人……都包在爱人如己这一句话之内了。爱是不加害与邻舍的，所以爱就完全了律法。"

基督扩展了"邻舍"的范围——路加福音10:25-37好撒马利亚人的比喻。律法师问"谁是我的邻舍？"——利未记19:18的"邻舍"在犹太传统中通常被限定为以色列同胞。耶稣用一个撒马利亚人（犹太人最鄙视的外邦人）来回答这个问题——你的邻舍是任何需要你帮助的人，不分种族、不分信仰。

"爱外人如己"（34节）——基督自己就是那位从天上"寄居"到地上的"外人"。约翰福音1:11"他到自己的地方来，自己的人倒不接待他。"基督经历了被排斥——所以祂能体恤每一个"寄居者"。希伯来书13:2"不可忘记用爱心接待客旅；因为曾有接待客旅的，不知不觉就接待了天使。"

"田角和落穗留给穷人"——基督自己就是那被留下的"田角"。祂被世界遗弃、被人厌弃——但正是这位被"留在地里"的，成了穷人和罪人的食粮。约翰福音6:35"耶稣说：我就是生命的粮。到我这里来的，必定不饿。"

## Christ's Shadow (English)

Leviticus 19:18 "love your neighbor as yourself" is the direct source of Christ's ethical teaching.

Jesus identified it as one of the greatest commandments—Matthew 22:37-40: "You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart...This is the great and first commandment. And a second is like it: You shall love your neighbor as yourself. On these two commandments depend all the Law and the Prophets." Brothers and sisters, Jesus elevated Leviticus 19:18 to equal standing with Deuteronomy 6:5 (love God)—all law hangs on these two.

Paul confirmed this—Galatians 5:14: "The whole law is fulfilled in one word: 'You shall love your neighbor as yourself.'" Romans 13:9-10: "The commandments, 'You shall not commit adultery, You shall not murder'...are summed up in this word: 'You shall love your neighbor as yourself.' Love does no wrong to a neighbor; therefore love is the fulfilling of the law."

Christ expanded "neighbor's" scope—Luke 10:25-37, the Good Samaritan parable. A lawyer asked "Who is my neighbor?"—Leviticus 19:18's "neighbor" was traditionally limited in Jewish interpretation to fellow Israelites. Jesus answered with a Samaritan (the Gentile Jews most despised)—your neighbor is anyone needing your help, regardless of race or faith.

"Love the stranger as yourself" (v. 34)—Christ Himself was the "stranger" who came from heaven to "sojourn" on earth. John 1:11: "He came to his own, and his own people did not receive him." Christ experienced rejection—so He could empathize with every "sojourner." Hebrews 13:2: "Do not neglect to show hospitality to strangers, for thereby some have entertained angels unawares."

"Field corners and gleanings for the poor"—Christ Himself was the "field corner" left behind. Rejected by the world, despised by men—yet this One "left in the field" became food for the poor and sinners. John 6:35: "Jesus said to them, 'I am the bread of life; whoever comes to me shall not hunger.'"
